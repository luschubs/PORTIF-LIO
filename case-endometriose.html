The Problem

Lead generation in a sensitive healthcare context.

A specialized endometriosis clinic in Brasília needed a landing page to capture qualified leads — women researching information about endometriosis, adenomyosis and fibroids — through a top-of-funnel content offer (an educational e-book).

The page would run as part of a Google Ads campaign, with traffic coming from informational search intent. The challenge wasn't writing — it was visual hierarchy that converts without exploiting an emotionally vulnerable audience.

My Role

UI design focused on conversion.

I was responsible for the UI design — visual hierarchy, layout, color system, form architecture, and CTA design. The copy was provided by the clinic's content team, so my work focused on translating their message into a structure that drives action without feeling pushy or commercial.

Design Decisions

Three choices that drove the result.

01

Hierarchy over decoration

The dominant color (gold/amber) draws the eye to two things: the headline and the CTA. Everything else is secondary. No visual noise competing for attention.

02

Form positioning

Form placed below social proof ("1 in 10 women") to convert curiosity into action. Field count was minimized to reduce friction without losing qualification.

03

Calm, not urgent

Warm color palette and rounded shapes — no countdown timers or red CTAs. The audience is anxious about their bodies; the page should feel reassuring.

The 27% conversion rate sits well above industry benchmarks for healthcare e-book landing pages (typically 15–25%). More important than the peak: the rate stayed consistent across 5 months — proving the design works regardless of seasonal traffic shifts.

Reflection

Calm design for anxious audiences.

This project taught me that conversion design and emotional design aren't opposites. The page converts because it respects the user — not despite respecting them.

Healthcare design has a real tension: you need leads, but you're talking to someone potentially scared about their body. The solution wasn't to soften the CTA — it was to remove everything that wasn't necessary, so the action felt natural instead of pushed.
